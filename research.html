Research

Arithmetic fluency across
memory, learning, and the brain.

My research examines why some people are more fluent in simple multiplication than others, and how behavioural performance reflects deeper cognitive and neurobiological processes.

Research Overview

My doctoral research explored single-digit multiplication fluency through a connected series of sociodemographic, cognitive, and neural investigations. The broader goal was to understand this foundational arithmetic skill more holistically in young adults, especially within the Malaysian context.

Across the thesis, I examined how demographic background, short-term memory, interference, cultural context, and neural recruitment each contribute to fluent performance. Taken together, these studies support the view that arithmetic fluency reflects an interaction between learning history, cognitive resources, and the efficiency of retrieval-related mechanisms.

Research Themes

01

Multiplication Fluency

I investigate single-digit multiplication fluency as a rich case for understanding automaticity, retrieval, interference, and individual differences in arithmetic skill.

02

Memory and Magnitude

A major strand of my work studies how verbal and visual short-term memory, as well as magnitude processing, shape multiplication performance in different learner populations.

03

Brain and Behaviour

I use neuroimaging and electrophysiological methods to relate arithmetic fluency to neural structure, conflict processing, and brain-behaviour relationships.

Selected Studies

Study 1

Demographic predictors of multiplication fluency

A survey-based study of 900 Malaysian undergraduates examining how language, educational background, ethnicity, and self-perceived learning experiences relate to arithmetic fluency.

Study 2

Short-term memory and multiplication performance

Behavioural work showing a dissociation between verbal and visual short-term memory, with lower verbal capacity linked to greater interference and higher visual capacity supporting larger-problem solving.

Study 3

Magnitude, physical size, and fact retrieval

An experiment testing size-congruity effects to show that highly fluent individuals rely more on automated fact retrieval and less on broader control processes during multiplication.

Study 4

Cross-cultural differences in multiplication fluency

A Malaysia-UK comparison showing stronger short-term memory performance and faster large-problem responding among Malaysian students, highlighting the role of cultural and educational context.

Study 5

Grey-matter volume and multiplication fluency

A voxel-based morphometry study showed that participants with higher timed multiplication fluency had greater grey-matter volume in the right opercular-insular cortex, supporting the idea that fluent performance depends partly on task-set maintenance, monitoring, and interference control.

Related Work

Electrophysiological studies of arithmetic processing

Alongside the studies included in my thesis, I have contributed to ERP and EEG research on arithmetic verification and the unfolding time course of multiplication-related processing. This work extends the neurocognitive side of my research programme by using electrophysiological methods to examine how problem structure and individual differences shape mathematical performance.

Future Research Interests

In addition to multiplication fluency, I am interested in broader questions about how people learn, why some individuals develop fluent skills more readily than others, and how rigorous quantitative methods can be used to study these differences across educational and applied settings.

I am especially open to collaborative work in bilingualism and arithmetic learning, cognitive development, learning-related individual differences, and methodologically grounded research in educational or training contexts.
