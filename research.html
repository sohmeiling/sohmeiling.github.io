Research

How arithmetic fluency emerges across memory, learning history, and neural systems.

My research examines why some people are more fluent in simple multiplication than others, and how behavioural performance reflects deeper cognitive and neurobiological processes.

Research Themes

01

Multiplication Fluency

I investigate single-digit multiplication fluency as a rich case for understanding automaticity, retrieval, interference, and individual differences in arithmetic skill.

02

Memory and Magnitude

A major strand of my work studies how verbal and visual short-term memory, as well as magnitude processing, shape multiplication performance in different learner populations.

03

Brain and Behaviour

I use neuroimaging and electrophysiological methods to relate arithmetic fluency to neural structure, conflict processing, and brain-behaviour relationships.

Possible Research Interests

In addition to my current work on multiplication fluency, I am interested in broader questions about how people learn, why some individuals develop fluent skills more readily than others, and how rigorous quantitative methods can be used to study these differences across educational and applied settings.

I am especially open to collaborative research in bilingualism and arithmetic learning, cognitive development, learning-related individual differences, and the use of psychological and quantitative methods in applied educational or training settings. These directions build naturally on my background in cognitive psychology, statistical analysis, and research training.

Methods

Behavioural

Large sociodemographic surveys, cognitive tasks, and controlled arithmetic experiments.

Neuroimaging

fMRI analysis with SPM12, design support with AFNI, visualization with MRIcroGL, EEG acquisition and preprocessing.

Analysis

Linear mixed-effects modelling in R using tidyverse, lme4, afex, and ggplot2.

Project Directions

My doctoral work developed across five manuscripts exploring memory predictors, language-of-learning effects, demographic factors, interference during multiplication solving, and neural correlates of fluent performance. Together, these studies build a more integrated account of arithmetic fluency.

Current Questions

Question 1

How do language, ethnicity, and learning history predict arithmetic fluency?

This work examines whether multiplication performance reflects broad educational and social differences, beyond simple bivariate associations.

Question 2

What cognitive processes are involved when multiplication conflicts with competing information?

This line of work looks at intentional and automatic processes using numerical Stroop-style paradigms.

Question 3

Which neural features are associated with higher multiplication fluency?

Ongoing neuroimaging work explores fluency-related differences in grey matter volume and interference sensitivity.
